          <h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://charlottelawyer.typepad.com/blog/2011/02/check-out-your-tax-preparer-.html">Check out your tax preparer -- Try looking for a qualified and legitimate preparer</a></h3> 
          <div class="entry-content"> 
           <div class="entry-body"> 
            <p>You've decided to hire someone to file your taxes this year. You've looked at your <a title="tax pro time - Don't Mess With Taxes" href="http://dontmesswithtaxes.typepad.com/dont_mess_with_taxes/2011/02/tax-pro-time.html" target="_blank">tax pro</a> options and decided which kind of paid preparer fits your needs.</p>
            <p>Now the next step: Thoroughly vetting the tax professional.</p>
            <p>Remember, regardless of who fills out your 1040 and sends it on to the IRS, when you sign your tax return, you are legally responsible for everything on it. So it's crucial to hire a tax pro who's not only knowledgeable, but also ethical.</p>
            <p>Most tax preparers are honest and provide quality service. But as with any consumer transaction, it's up to you, the buyer of the services, to make sure you're hiring a true professional.</p>
            <p>Here are some things to keep in mind when someone else prepares your return.</p>
            <p><strong>Check on IRS registration</strong><br />Starting this year, preparers must be <a title="IRS.gov urges paid preparers to register" href="http://www.irs.gov/efile/article/0,,id=118449,00.html" target="_blank">registered with the IRS</a> to file a return. Make sure the person you hire has done so.</p>
            <p><strong>Complaints count</strong><br />Look into whether the preparer has any questionable history with the Better Business Bureau. Also check your state's board of accountancy for CPAs, your state's bar association for attorneys or the IRS Office of Professional Responsibility for Enrolled Agents.</p>
            <p>If you live in a state that licenses or registers tax preparers, contact that regulatory office to make sure there are no complaints against the person you plan to hire.</p>
            <p><strong>Do they belong?</strong><br />Check your preparers professional affiliations. Membership in such groups requires that a tax preparer follow a code of ethics and participate in continuing education programs.</p>
            <p><strong>Office hours are important</strong><br />Make sure your tax preparer will be around after the April filing deadline. Some offices open up just during filing season and then disappear as soon as returns are filed. But the IRS can come asking questions about a return months later. The person who helped you file it should be around so you can get the answers to any possible IRS questions.</p>
            <p><strong>Who does the actual work?</strong><br />If your tax preparer is part of larger firm, determine whether he or she will be the person actually doing your tax work or whether your taxes will be delegated to someone else in the office, perhaps a person with less tax training.</p>
            <p>Also find out if your preparer exports tax returns for preparation. Foreign countries do not have the same security and privacy laws as the United States, nor is there any recourse should your information be compromised as a result of lax or nonexistent privacy procedures.</p>
            <p><strong>How do they charge?</strong><br />Don't hire a tax preparer whose fee is based on how big of a refund you get. That's usually an indication that tax law envelopes will be pushed to get you more money than you're rightfully entitled to just so your tax preparer can make more money.</p>
            <p>Similarly, avoid preparers who claim they can get you a larger refund than other preparers. If your returns are prepared correctly, every preparer should come up with essentially the same tax and refund numbers.</p>
            <p><strong>Find someone who wants to know</strong><br />Be wary of preparers who aren't concerned about documentation. Reputable tax preparers will request to see your receipts and will ask you detailed questions to determine your qualifications for expenses, deductions, credits and the like. It's always better that these questions are asked by your preparer and answered before filing, rather than later by an IRS examiner.</p>
            <p><strong>Avoid blank return requests</strong><br />Remember that warning about being responsible for the return you sign? If a tax preparer asks you to sign a blank tax return, find a new tax pro. Never sign a tax document before it's finished and then only after you've read it carefully and understand the entries.</p>
            <p>If you're unsure about an entry and your tax pro can't explain it to your satisfaction, then don't sign the return. A tax preparer who won't or can't explain the items on your 1040 and associated schedules is either incompetent or unscrupulous.</p>
            <p>Sure, thoroughly checking out your tax pro takes some time. But it's time well spent when it ensures that your taxes are completed properly.</p>

          <h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://charlottelawyer.typepad.com/blog/2011/01/delay-means-time-crunch-for-some-taxpayer-returns.html">Delay means time crunch for some taxpayer returns</a></h3> 
          <div class="entry-content"> 
           <div class="entry-body"> 
            <p>posted by <a href="http://concordnclawyer.com/taxes.html">low cost tax preparation</a></p>
            <p><a href="mailto:lpowers@rgj.com">By Lenita Powers</a> • lpowers@rgj.com • January 29, 2011</p>
            <p>Changes approved late last year by Congress have pushed back the date when taxpayers who claim itemized deductions can begin filing their income tax returns, resulting in headaches for accountants and possibly a longer wait for Americans who expect refunds.</p>
            <p>The tax-filing period began Jan. 4, but taxpayers claiming itemized deductions -- which includes almost everyone with a mortgage -- can't send in their federal tax returns until Feb. 14.</p>
            <p>Legislation to extend former President George W. Bush's tax cuts through 2012 and other tax breaks, which were set to expire, including college tuition and the state and local sales tax deduction that benefits Nevada, weren't passed by Congress until Dec. 17.</p>
            <p>&quot;It was so late in the year, we needed to get our systems up to speed in order to be able to accept those tax returns that had deductions related to what was in the new law,&quot; said Raphael Tulino, Internal Revenue Service spokesman for Nevada and Southern California</p>
            <p>Out of an estimated 50 million federal tax returns, only about 9 million with itemized deductions will be affected by the changes, Tulino said.</p>
            <p>That 9 million represents a small number of taxpayers who will be affected compared with the 140 million tax returns expected to be filed this year.</p>
            <p>&quot;Only a third of the country itemizes,&quot; he said. &quot;So this is not affecting a majority of the taxpayers. Don't get me wrong; if you're one of those who wanted to file ASAP, we apologize. But we wanted to have those systems reprogrammed so taxpayers get their returns properly processed and we needed that extra time because of all the changes that came our way.&quot;</p>
            <p>The upside of any delay or confusion for taxpayers who itemize is that they will be able to keep those deductions that had been set to expire and get bigger refunds.</p>
            <p>The downside is taxpayers who prepare their own tax returns or accountants who do it for them have one month less to prepare federal tax returns, cutting their window of work down to about three months this year unless they file for extensions.</p>
            <p>&quot;It's thrown a wrench into it,&quot; said Michael Bosma, a certified public accountant and managing shareholder with the Bosma Group in Reno. &quot;A lot of individuals' returns we file, they usually get the information for those returns to us in late January or early February. With this delay, they can't file them until the second week in February.</p>
            <p>&quot;So now clients are thinking they don't have to get their stuff to us until later,&quot; he said. &quot;That bunches up our work so we have to reach out to clients and say, 'Please, please, get us the information as soon as possible so we can prepare your return and then file it as soon as we are able to file on Feb. 14.&quot;</p>
            <p>The deadline to get your taxes to the IRS this year also has been pushed back by three days. Tax returns have to be postmarked by midnight April 18 instead of April 15 because of Emancipation Day, which is being observed in Washington, D.C., falls on April 15, a Friday. By law, holidays observed in the District of Columbia are comparable to a federal holiday, which pushed the tax deadline this year to April 18, a Monday.</p>
            <p>&quot;That's another maddening part of this,&quot; said Bosma, who has clients spread across the United States and some who are international. &quot;The IRS has delayed when you can send in your return, but there is no corresponding delay on the filing deadline. So you end up with a couple of weeks less to get the same volume of work done.&quot;</p>
            <p>Deane Albright, senior partner with Albright, Persing and Associates in Reno, doesn't think the loss of one month to get clients' taxes prepared and sent to the IRS will make that much difference.</p>
            <p>&quot;By the time most people get their W-2s, 1099s and all that type of information gathered up, they normally don't have all that in until close to Feb. 14 anyway,&quot; said Albright, whose CPA firm has 20 people who handle individual, business and corporate taxes and audits. &quot;So the delay is not that significant.&quot;</p>
            <p>Taxpayers who use short-form or simple tax returns with no itemized deductions can send their taxes in before Feb. 14, he said.</p>
            <p>For those who do itemize deductions, Albright agrees with Bosma that they still need to get the information to the accountant or business that prepares their taxes as soon as possible before the Feb. 14 crunch &quot;so it will be ready to go and they will know what their refund amount will be.&quot;</p>
            <p>Tulino said taxpayers filing returns with itemized deductions won't have to wait longer to get their refunds even though they have two weeks less to file their tax returns.</p>
            <p>Bosma disagrees.</p>
            <p>&quot;This will delay the refunds to those taxpayers who filed for itemized deductions,&quot; Bosma said.</p>
            <p>&quot;If you file your return two weeks later than usual, that means it will take two weeks longer to get it. If the IRS feels confident that it's not going to happen, that's awesome, but in my mind, it will still take four to six weeks from the day they get your return to process it. I don't think the IRS has the infrastructure to get your refund back to you in two weeks' less time than it usually takes. They have not lessened the turnaround time for your refund and this year, some taxpayers have lost two weeks that they could have used to get those returns in earlier.&quot;</p>
            <p>Bosma said the tax code changes and the delay will not affect most businesses, which had very few credits impacted.</p>
            <p>Ben Steele of Steele and Associates, a CPA firm in Carson City, said the delay in the tax filing date is causing problems for businesses, especially if they have partnerships in some other states.</p>
            <p>&quot;There are deadlines in some states for certain things like farming that have to be in by Feb. 15,&quot; Steele said. &quot;And all state income taxes relate to the federal income tax, so if you deal with multiple states like we do, you have issues. The federal tax programs are pretty much driving the state programs because of all the changes the IRS has to make.&quot;</p>

          <h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://charlottelawyer.typepad.com/blog/2011/01/filing-bankruptcy-can-keep-the-creditors-from-harassing-you.html">Filing Bankruptcy Can Keep The Creditors From Harassing You</a></h3> 
          <div class="entry-content"> 
           <div class="entry-body"> 
            <p>posted by&nbsp; <a href="http://maxwelllegal.com">charlotte chapter 7</a></p> 
            <p>17.01.2011 | Author: <a href="http://www.freeezinearticles.com/author/smithsbryan/">smithsbryan</a> | Posted in <a href="http://www.freeezinearticles.com/category/finance/debt-consolidation/">Debt Consolidation</a></p> 
            <p>Regardless of what your creditors may have led you to believe, you have more power than you think when it comes to your finances. You need to learn more about filing bankruptcy and how the automatic stay may give you comforting protection from creditors that you haven’t had in a very long time. If you’ve grown tired of the late-night collection calls from angry creditors and then having to explain to your kids why you’re so upset after getting off the phone, take action now before it’s too late.</p> 
            <p>There is a lot of important information that a person should be aware of whether they are looking specifically for personal bankruptcy information or business bankruptcy information.           <h3 class="entry-header"><a href="http://charlottelawyer.typepad.com/blog/2011/01/student-loan-bankruptcy.html">Student Loan Bankruptcy</a></h3> 
          <div class="entry-content"> 
           <div class="entry-body"> 
            <p>posted by <a href="http://concordnclawyer.com" title="Chapter 7 concord nc bankruptcy">chapter 7 concord north carolina </a></p>
            <p>from http://loans.dracony.org/2011/01/15/student-loan-bankruptcy/</p>
            <p><strong>Student Loans Aren’t Discharged by Bankruptcy</strong><br /> That’s the bad news. Due to bankruptcy reforms in 1998 and 2005, it’s almost impossible for the average person to discharge federal or private student loans through bankruptcy. You may be able to get help with your payments through a bankruptcy filing, but there are better options for repaying your student loans.</p>
            <p><strong>The Student Loan Bankruptcy Exception</strong><br /> As with all rules there is one exception: you can discharge a student loan in bankruptcy due to undue hardship. Undue hardship is defined as the permanent physical inability to work. You must prove in bankruptcy court that:</p>
            <p>You’re physically unable to work You’re likely to be unable to work for most of the loan term You’ve made a good faith effort to repay the debt Paying it would prevent you, your spouse, and your dependents from maintaining a “minimal” standard of living.</p>
            <p>If you believe you qualify under these guidelines, see an experienced bankruptcy lawyer for help filing an adversary proceeding as part of your bankruptcy case.</p>
            <p><strong>How Bankruptcy Can Help with Student Loans</strong><br /> Although your student loan can’t be discharged in bankruptcy, a bankruptcy court may be able to ease an overwhelming debt burden. Some courts may discharge a portion of your student loans, but this is rare and varies by court.</p>
            <p>In most cases, the judge will incorporate your student loans into your debt repayment plan under Chapter 13 bankruptcy. Any balance remaining after the payment plan ends will still be due, but your other debts should be paid off by then.</p>
            <p><strong>What to Do if You’re Heading Toward Bankruptcy</strong><br /> If your total debts have reached an unsustainable level and you feel you must file for bankruptcy, don’t simply stop paying your student loans. Not only are student loans not dischargeable in bankruptcy, but also the federal government has the right to assess stiff penalties, seize tax refunds and other government assistance money, and garnish your wages.</p>
            <p>Lenders want to help you avoid default. Contact them for help applying for a deferral, forbearance, or extended repayment plan before the situation gets worse than it already is.</p>
            <p><strong>Solutions for Student Loans You Don’t Owe</strong><br /> If a lender is demanding payment for a student loan you don’t think you owe, it’s best to resolve the situation before you wind up in bankruptcy court.</p>
            <p>The most typical situation is a miscalculation of the actual loan balance, especially if the loan has changed lenders multiple times. If you think the lender is requesting more than you owe or hasn’t properly credited payments, write to them with your evidence. If the issue is not resolved, then a court can intervene to determine the amount you actually owe. A bankruptcy judge may also do this as part of a bankruptcy proceeding.</p>
            <p>Your debt may be cancelled if a few situations apply:<br />Situation 1: Your school closed before you completed your education and you couldn’t complete it elsewhere. You don’t qualify if you voluntarily withdrew before the school closed. You may be entitled to a loan reduction if you voluntarily withdrew and the school improperly withheld any remaining student loan funds.</p>
            <p>Situation 2: Your school or another party signed the promissory note in your name without your approval or the school falsely certified you as eligible for a student loan when you were not.</p>
            <p>Situation 3: You were forced to withdraw due to a disability that developed while you were in school, or that certifiably worsened after you accepted the loan.</p>
            <p>For all three situations, it’s best to contact the lender or the federal student loan program for assistance in resolving your unowed debts. Although a bankruptcy court can sort it out for you, other solutions are simpler and better for your financial future.</p>
            <p><strong>Student Loan Cancellation Programs</strong><br /> Several federal and state agencies offer programs to help you cancel or reduce all or a portion of your student loan debt without filing for bankruptcy. Most programs involve teaching, nursing, or military service.</p>
            <p>In most cases, bankruptcy won’t erase your student loans. Although bankruptcy is still a viable solution for desperate financial situations, it’s best for your future financial well being to avoid it. Contact your lenders as soon as a problem develops in order to avoid worse financial repercussions.</p>
